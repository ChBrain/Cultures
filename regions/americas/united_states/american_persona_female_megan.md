# Persona: Megan

## Title: Emergency Room Nurse

## Owner
- Project: Cultures
- Culture: United States

## Projection
Megan is a [woman](../../../engine/position_female.md) from the [United States](culture_american_position.md). She is a registered nurse in the emergency department of a 280-bed community hospital outside Akron, Ohio. Born 1989, BSN from a state university in 2011, married a fellow nurse in 2016, divorced in 2022, no kids, lives in a two-bedroom rental ten minutes from the hospital, drives a 2019 Honda CR-V she bought after the divorce. She works three twelves: Friday, Saturday, Sunday, seven to seven, swing weeks rotating to nights.

[American English](culture_american_position_language.md) is her only language at depth. The hospital's medical Spanish she keeps on a laminated card in her badge holder, the lines that triage uses: *señala donde le duele, en una escala del uno al diez, cuándo empezó*. She [reads the card and follows it word for word](../../../engine/process_reading_followed.md); the patient's grandmother answers in a dialect the card was not written for, and she calls the language line. Her charting she [assembles from templates and checks line by line](../../../engine/process_writing_copied.md): *Patient is a 47 y/o female who presents to the ED with...* The shift handoff at change-of-shift she [follows the content of](../../../engine/process_hearing_followed.md), seven beds in seven minutes, the off-going nurse running it like a play.

She lives ninety miles from the closest piece of the country the textbook calls historical: the Cuyahoga Valley, where the river caught fire in 1969 and started the Clean Water Act. The [history](culture_american_piece_history.md) she carries is more recent: the opioid epidemic she has watched come through her department in three waves since she started, the COVID lockdowns where she was in a respirator and three pairs of gloves for eighteen months. Her unit hangs the staff photos on a wall in the break room; one of the photos is of a nurse who died in 2020, and the unit does not take it down. She drives the [interstate](culture_american_piece_interstate.md) to and from work, exit 22 northbound, exit 21 southbound. She has been to [Washington, DC](culture_american_place_washington_dc.md) twice, both times for the National Nurses March.

## Action
The shift is on its sixth hour and the waiting room is at thirty-one. Bed 4 is a chest pain, sixty-eight, history of CAD, on the monitor with troponin pending. Bed 7 is an MVA from the [interstate](culture_american_piece_interstate.md), a teenager with a broken ankle and a worried mom. Bed 9 is the regular, alcohol withdrawal, third visit this month, asleep.
Megan goes to bed 7 first. The mom is the loudest person in the unit and the kid is in the most pain.
She gets the kid four of morphine, splints the ankle the way the orthopedic resident showed her in 2014, calls the X-ray tech, charts the vitals, prints the discharge instructions before the doc has written them.
On the way out of bed 7 she ducks into bed 4 to read the new troponin off the monitor and ducks into bed 9 to check that the regular is still breathing. She does not chart either of those visits. She does not tell anyone she did them.
At the end of the shift the charge nurse asks if anything was held over. Megan says no.
She does not say that she carried the unit through hour six.
She drives home on the [interstate](culture_american_piece_interstate.md) and stops at the gas station for a sweet tea.

## Shadow
Megan thinks she is staying out of the spotlight because nursing is teamwork and the unit runs on everyone.
What she does not see is that she has built a record of being the nurse who handles the heavy load and does not [pitch](culture_american_process_pitching.md) it back as a story. The charge nurse knows. The doc knows. Three years running, the new hires get oriented to her shifts because she will catch their mistakes. She is paid the same as the new hires after their first raise. The hospital is not going to volunteer the promotion to her if she does not ask, and she is not going to ask because asking is not what good nurses do.
She believes she is being humble. The hospital reads humility as availability and moves on. She is the foundation of the unit and she is invisible to the org chart, and she has built that condition with her own hands every Friday, Saturday, Sunday for a decade.

## Tell
At change-of-shift, when the off-going charge asks if she had anything held over, her left hand goes flat on the counter at the nurses' station, fingers spread, while she says *nope, we're good*, before the charge has finished the question.

# Place: Washington, DC

## Owner
- Project: Cultures
- Culture: United States

## Shown
The Capitol on the hill, white sandstone and marble, the dome on top finished during the Civil War while the war was still being fought.
The National Mall as a long lawn, two miles from the Capitol steps to the Lincoln Memorial, with the Washington Monument in the middle, 555 feet of pointed stone.
The Reflecting Pool between the Lincoln and the Washington, water that shows the buildings to themselves.
The White House on Pennsylvania Avenue, the address everybody knows by number: 1600.
The Supreme Court across the street from the Capitol, *EQUAL JUSTICE UNDER LAW* on the pediment.
The Smithsonian museums on both sides of the Mall, free of charge, no ticket, walk in.
The Vietnam Veterans Memorial, a black granite V cut into the ground, 58,000 names in the order they fell.
The Pentagon across the Potomac, five sides, five rings, the largest office building in the world.
The neighborhoods past the Mall: Georgetown's federal-style brick, Anacostia east of the river, the embassies up Massachusetts Avenue, the row houses of Capitol Hill.

## Holds
The [American](culture_american_position.md) position in its institutional form.
The [history](culture_american_piece_history.md), inked into the founding documents at the National Archives a block off the Mall: Declaration, Constitution, Bill of Rights, behind argon-and-helium glass.
The First Amendment, applied daily on the Mall by anyone with a permit (and often without one).
The federal government as the daily employer of about 350,000 people in the metropolitan area, the largest single employer in the region. Title and seniority and protocol live in the office buildings off the Mall, in the chain of senator, senior staff, and the policy shop downstream.
Congress in session, the Court in term, the President in the West Wing, the press corps in the briefing room.

## Offers
You can stand on the Lincoln Memorial steps where King stood on August 28, 1963, and read the speech where it was given.
You can sit in the gallery of the House or the Senate when they are in session, walk in off the street, watch the floor.
You can file a Freedom of Information Act request, by name, with a federal agency, and get a docket number back.
You can [pitch](culture_american_process_pitching.md) the staffer who pitches the senator, on the sidewalk between the Capitol and the office buildings, on a first-name basis whether or not the senator knows you.
You can lobby, march, protest, hold a press conference, write to your representative, and use the city as the venue for all of it: it was built to be the venue. The federal procedure is documented; the workaround is also documented.

## Withheld
Statehood. The District has no voting representation in the Senate and one non-voting delegate in the House. About 700,000 people, taxed, drafted, and governed by a Congress they cannot fully vote for. *END TAXATION WITHOUT REPRESENTATION* is printed on the license plates.
The peace. The city is a permanent fight: every administration arrives ready to undo the last one, every Congress flips, every Court term ends with at least one decision the country has not finished arguing about. The sense of national community is held together less by a shared belief and more by an experiment in argument.
The decisions of the next term. Whether the executive order will hold, whether the agency will be funded, whether the bill that died in committee will come back. The room is loud and the room is provisional.

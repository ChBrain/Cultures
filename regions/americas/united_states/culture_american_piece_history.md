# History: United States

## Owner
- Project: Cultures
- Culture: United States

## Place
North America, from the Atlantic to the Pacific, from the Great Lakes to the Rio Grande.
A continent annexed in pieces over two and a half centuries: thirteen colonies on the coast, then a purchase from France, then a war with Mexico, then a treaty with Britain over Oregon, then a check written to the Russian tsar for Alaska.
Fifty states today, plus the District of Columbia, plus territories the maps print smaller than they are.
The frontier line moved west until the Census Bureau closed it in 1890, and then it moved into the head and stayed there.

## Load Bearing
Remove the founding documents and the [American](culture_american_position.md) position has no contract under it.
Remove the Civil War and the federal floor under personal liberty falls out.
Remove the New Deal and the social contract of the twentieth century has no spine.
Remove the civil rights movement and the First Amendment is a promissory note no one ever cashed.
The country is unusually young for a country that talks about itself this much, and it spends a lot of [language](culture_american_position_language.md) on reading the founding texts back to itself, in school, in court, on the floor of Congress.
The deal-as-unit, the second chance, the open field of reinvention: each is written down somewhere, in a document with a date, and a date with a Yearbook entry.
The history is read forward, not back: the present is the test of the founding, and the founders are graded daily on whether their words still hold.

## Apparent
The Declaration of Independence behind glass at the National Archives in [Washington, DC](culture_american_place_washington_dc.md), the ink faded to where the signatures are studied by experts.
The Constitution in the case next to it. The Bill of Rights in the case after.
The Star-Spangled Banner at the Smithsonian, the actual flag from Fort McHenry, fifteen stars, fifteen stripes.
The Lincoln Memorial, twenty-five feet of seated marble, the Gettysburg Address on one wall and the Second Inaugural on the other.
The Vietnam Veterans Memorial Wall, names cut into black granite in the order they fell.
The Statue of Liberty in New York Harbor, lit at night since 1886, holding the date of the Declaration in the book.
The interstate shield on the green sign, the route number inside it, the network laid out since 1956.
The flag in the schoolroom, the pledge before the bell, the Pledge of Allegiance amended in 1954 to add *under God*.
Independence Day on the fourth of July, fireworks in the public park, the grill in every backyard that has one; the long weekend that bookends the summer vacation.

## Yearbook
1607: Jamestown. The first permanent English settlement on the continent, on a tidal river in Virginia, named for the king.
1620: Mayflower lands at Plymouth. The Mayflower Compact, signed in the cabin, is the country's first written agreement to govern by mutual consent.
1776: Declaration of Independence, July 4, Philadelphia. *We hold these truths to be self-evident.* The right to *life, liberty and the pursuit of happiness* is written into the founding sentence.
1787: Constitution drafted in Philadelphia, ratified 1788, in force 1789. *We the People* in three words larger than the rest.
1791: Bill of Rights ratified, the first ten amendments, the First Amendment guaranteeing speech, press, assembly, religion, and the right to petition the government.
1803: Louisiana Purchase. Jefferson buys 828,000 square miles from Napoleon for fifteen million dollars; the country doubles overnight, as a sustained federal investment in territory.
1861-1865: Civil War. Eleven states secede; the war kills about 620,000 Americans, more than every other American war combined until Vietnam. Lee surrenders at Appomattox on April 9, 1865.
1863: Emancipation Proclamation, January 1; Gettysburg Address, November 19. Lincoln rewrites the founding in 272 words: *of the people, by the people, for the people.*
1865: Thirteenth Amendment, ratified December 6. Slavery abolished by federal statute.
1869: Transcontinental Railroad completed at Promontory Summit, Utah, May 10. The golden spike. The coasts are five days apart, not five months.
1890: Census Bureau declares the frontier line closed. Frederick Jackson Turner reads his paper on the meaning of it three years later in Chicago.
1920: Nineteenth Amendment ratified, August 18. Women vote nationally for the first time.
1929: Wall Street Crash, October 24-29. Black Tuesday. The Great Depression follows.
1933: New Deal begins. Roosevelt's first hundred days. The federal government takes on relief, recovery, reform as its daily job.
1941: Pearl Harbor attacked December 7. *A date which will live in infamy.* The country enters World War II the next day.
1944: D-Day, June 6. Allied landings at Normandy. American divisions on Omaha and Utah beaches.
1954: Brown v. Board of Education, May 17. The Supreme Court declares segregated schools unconstitutional; *separate but equal* overturned.
1963: March on Washington, August 28. King's *I Have a Dream* speech from the steps of the Lincoln Memorial.
1964: Civil Rights Act. Title VII bans employment discrimination by race, color, religion, sex, national origin.
1969: Apollo 11 lands on the Moon, July 20. Armstrong's first step. *One small step for [a] man, one giant leap for mankind.*
1973: Roe v. Wade, January 22. The Supreme Court recognizes a constitutional right to abortion (overturned in Dobbs, 2022).
1989: Berlin Wall falls, November 9. Americans read it as the end of the Cold War their country has run for forty years.
2001: September 11. Hijacked airliners strike the World Trade Center towers and the Pentagon; United 93 goes down in a Pennsylvania field. About 2,977 killed; the immediate response is the Patriot Act, signed October 26.
2008: Lehman Brothers files for bankruptcy, September 15. The financial crisis follows. Obama elected in November, first Black president.
2020: COVID-19 pandemic; lockdowns in March. The country closes the schools, the bars, and the stadium. Over a million Americans die before the count stops being daily news.

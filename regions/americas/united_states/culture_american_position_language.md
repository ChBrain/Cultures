# Language: American English

## Owner
- Project: Cultures
- Culture: United States

## Has
American English is the language of the contract and the language of the diner counter, and the speaker is expected to move between them without ceremony. The President is addressed by title; the senator's chief of staff is addressed as Mike. The cashier asks how you are doing, and "fine, thanks, you" is not a question about wellbeing, it is the password for the next ten seconds.

"You" is universal. There is no formal *Sie*, no *vous*. The boss says you, the customer says you, the cop says you. The South recovers a second person plural in *y'all*; the upper Midwest borrows *you guys*; New York and Philly use *youse*. The standard form refuses the distinction and pays for it with the workaround.

Contractions carry the everyday register. *I'm, you're, we'll, don't, wouldn't've.* The full forms are reserved for the page where the lawyer is reading: *I am, you are, we will, do not.* Switching to the long form mid-sentence is itself a tell, a shift into the formal register, often a shift into trouble.

The spelling is the divergence Noah Webster made in 1828: *color, honor, labor, center, theater, organize, analyze, traveled, defense, check, tire, curb, program.* The British forms read as foreign or as affectation, even when used by an American writer; the divergence is no longer about ideology, it is about which page you are on. *Schedule* rhymes with *sked*; *aluminum* has four syllables; *vitamin* has a long *i*.

The legal register sits at the other end. The Constitution writes *We the People*; the contract writes *the Parties*; the indictment writes *the Defendant*. The Anglo-Norman doublets carry into American statute: *cease and desist, null and void, will and testament, aid and abet, terms and conditions.* The motion is filed in the language of 1791 and the deal is closed in the language of the diner. The federal regulation reads in a tight procedure that admits no ambiguity, while the bar argues over the same words with informal tolerance for risk and flexible, loose interpretation.

Regional varieties are real but the standard is anchored by the network anchor, the wire-service reporter, and the airline pilot. General American is the broadcast voice, rhotic, mid-Atlantic-less, midwestern-flat by default. The Southern drawl, the New England *cah* without the *r*, the African American Vernacular with its aspectual *be* (*she be working* names the habitual, not the moment), the Chicano English of East LA, the Spanglish of Miami: each is a register the language carries without asking the speaker to apologize for it.

The vocabulary leans concrete and short. *Trunk*, not *boot*. *Hood*, not *bonnet*. *Sidewalk*, not *pavement*. *Apartment*, not *flat*. *Vacation*, not *holiday*. *Fall*, not *autumn*. *Gas*, not *petrol*. *Cell* (or *cell phone*), not *mobile*. *Cookie*, not *biscuit*. *Cilantro*, not *coriander*. *ZIP code*, not *postcode*. *Truck*, not *lorry*. *Elevator*, not *lift*. *French fries*, not *chips*. *Chips*, not *crisps*.

## Orders
Use the first-name address with peers.
Use the contraction at the bar; use the full verb in the [filing](culture_american_process_pitching.md).
Say it in five words if six would do.
Say *you* and let the room figure out which kind.

## Loses
The register that is neither casual nor courtroom. The middle voice that other languages mark and American English flattens: between the slap on the back and the *whereas, the Parties hereby agree*, there is not much soft ground. American English will move you from *howdy, partner* to *I'll see you in court* without an intermediate stop, and the speaker who wanted a middle register has to build it themselves with body and tone, because the words will not do it for them. Modesty is one of the casualties: the language has no easy way to say *I did this and I am not making a claim about it.* The speaker either says *I did this* (a claim) or stays quiet (read as nothing).

## Drives
On the loss of the middle register:
The persona learns to use the room to do the work the words will not. The handshake softens the deal; the second beer softens the disagreement; the first-name address softens the rank. The American room runs on tone-of-voice and body, because the language refuses to encode them. The persona who masters this can [pitch](culture_american_process_pitching.md) hard and stay friends; the persona who does not, sounds either cold or fake, and is shown the door, courteously, on a first-name basis. The tone of cooperation has to be carried in body and voice; the words by themselves will not signal care, compassion, or harmony.

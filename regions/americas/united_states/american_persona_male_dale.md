# Persona: Dale

## Title: HVAC Technician

## Owner
- Project: Cultures
- Culture: United States

## Projection
Dale is a [man](../../../engine/position_male.md) from the [United States](culture_american_position.md). He owns Dale's Heating & Air, a one-truck operation out of a suburb north of Houston. Born 1978, grew up on Bush Sr. and Clinton, married, two kids in middle school, mortgage on a four-bedroom off the feeder road of the [interstate](culture_american_piece_interstate.md). He drives a white Ford F-250 with his number on the side and a USMC sticker on the back glass from his two enlistments after high school.

[American English](culture_american_position_language.md) is the only language he reads, writes, speaks, and hears with any depth. Spanish he picks up off the jobsite, the words that matter on a roof in July: *frio, caliente, no funciona, mañana*. He [carries the Spanish like a wrench he keeps in the truck](../../../engine/process_speaking_carried.md) and uses it when the homeowner's grandmother answers the door. The customer's manuals he [reads in full and gets the whole hand](../../../engine/process_reading_caught.md), because they are written for him in the register he was trained in. The supplier's emails he [drafts plainly and goes](../../../engine/process_writing_drafted.md): *Mike - need 4 of the 410A on the truck Monday, thx, Dale.* The radio in the cab on the way to the next call he [follows the content of, not the undertone](../../../engine/process_hearing_followed.md): sports talk, the news at the top of the hour, the weather, the country station between calls.

He has a [daily round](../../../engine/process_world_is_spinning.md) which means he runs the route in his head the night before and starts the truck before he has finished the coffee. The [history](culture_american_piece_history.md) he carries is the Memorial Day cookout and the Fourth of July, not the textbook; he learned what he needed to know about the country from the Marines and from his grandfather, who is buried at the Houston National Cemetery, and from the [interstate](culture_american_piece_interstate.md) signs he passes on the way to every call. He has been to [Washington, DC](culture_american_place_washington_dc.md) once, on a school field trip in the eighth grade; he remembers the Lincoln Memorial and the way the security guard at the Capitol said *son*.

## Action
The work order says *AC out, won't cool*. The house is from 1994, builder-grade unit on the side, the kind he sees ten of a day in July.
Dale walks the lot first. He checks the condenser, listens for the compressor, puts the meter on the contactor, reads the pressures off the gauges. Forty minutes in, he has it: low-side suction is wrong, the evaporator coil is half-blocked with a decade of dog hair the homeowner never knew was there.
He could clean it and recharge it. The system is sixteen years old. The compressor is going to fail next summer, and he can hear it, the way the bearings whine when it cycles.
He tells the homeowner. He shows her the gauges. He writes two numbers on the back of the invoice: 380 for the patch, 6,200 for the replacement. He does not [pitch](culture_american_process_pitching.md) the replacement. He tells her the patch is the patch and the replacement is the replacement and either one is honest work.
She picks the patch. He cleans the coil, recharges the system, writes up the invoice, takes the check. On the way out he hands her his card.
"Call me first when it goes. I'll give you the wholesale on the unit. Labor's the same either way."

## Shadow
Dale believes he tells people the truth and lets them decide.
What he does not see is that he has decided already, every time, in the order he lays out the numbers. The patch goes first; the patch sounds reasonable; the patch is what the customer picks 80 percent of the time. He has been running this script for fifteen years and the numbers come out the same.
He believes the customer is in charge. The customer is in the room with him and his gauges and his twenty years of pattern matching, and the customer picks the option he made cheaper-sounding. He does not call this a sale. He calls it being straight with people.
He is, mostly. But the script is a sale anyway. And when his daughter, sixteen and looking at college, tells him she wants to go to nursing school out of state, he runs the same script on her, in the same order, and is surprised when she pushes back.

## Tell
When he hands the invoice across the kitchen counter, his free hand goes to the back of his neck and rubs it once, where the sun has cooked the skin into a permanent red, before the customer has had a chance to read the number.

# Piece: The Interstate Highway System

## Owner
- Project: Cultures
- Culture: United States

## Place
The continental United States, from Boston to San Diego, from Miami to Seattle.
About 48,890 miles of limited-access highway, signed with a red, white, and blue shield, numbered on a grid: odd numbers run north-south (I-5 on the Pacific, I-95 on the Atlantic), even numbers run east-west (I-10 along the southern border, I-90 across the north).
Two lanes each way at minimum, four or five through the metro areas, with shoulders on both sides and an emergency call box every mile, before the cell phone made them obsolete.
Officially the Dwight D. Eisenhower National System of Interstate and Defense Highways, since 1990. Nobody calls it that. They call it *the interstate*.

## Load Bearing
Remove the interstate and the [American](culture_american_position.md) suburb stops working: there is no commute to the cul-de-sac, no truck route to the strip mall, no Friday drive to the lake.
Remove the interstate and the American second chance loses its road: the U-Haul does not pull out of the driveway, the family does not arrive in Phoenix with the kids in the back, the man with the bad year does not start over in Denver.
The interstate carries the American [history](culture_american_piece_history.md) of mobility as personal liberty, not a privilege; you do not need a passport to leave the state, you do not check in with anyone, you fill the tank and you go.
It carries the federal-state contract: federally funded at 90 percent under the original 1956 act, built and maintained by the states, signed in a national standard so a truck loaded in Tacoma can be unloaded in Tampa without changing the rules of the road. It carries the federal investment as a long outlay that did not get paid back inside one administration.
It carries the American freight bill: more than 70 percent of US tonnage moves by truck.
*Made in USA* moves on it; the deal at the loading dock depends on it.

## Apparent
The green sign with white text, exit number top right, mileage to the next three cities below.
The red, white, and blue shield with the route number; the white shield with the black US route number for the older roads underneath; the state route on its own shape, varying by state.
The rest area, restrooms and vending machines, the picnic table under the trees, the map on the wall under glass with *YOU ARE HERE* in red.
The truck stop, the diesel pumps, the diner with bottomless coffee, the showers for the long-haul drivers, the parking lot full of rigs at 3 a.m.
The on-ramp's metering light, red and green, holding traffic back during rush hour in Los Angeles, Chicago, Atlanta.
The HOV lane, the diamond on the pavement, two or more occupants only.
The toll plaza on the I-95 corridor, the transponder beeping as the car passes the gantry without slowing.
The orange barrels of the construction zone, the *FINES DOUBLE IN WORK ZONES* sign, the flagger in the high-visibility vest.
The truck weigh station, *OPEN* or *CLOSED* in lights, the scale in the right lane; the federal inspection sticker the trucker hopes is current.
The state line sign, *WELCOME TO TEXAS*, *DRIVE FRIENDLY*, the documented mileage to the next exit posted underneath.

## Yearbook
1916: Federal Aid Road Act. The first federal money for state highways, a hundred million dollars matched by the states.
1919: Lieutenant Colonel Dwight Eisenhower joins the Army's first transcontinental motor convoy, Washington, DC to San Francisco. The trip takes 62 days. He remembers it.
1926: US Numbered Highway System adopted. Route 66 from Chicago to Santa Monica gets its number. The shield is the white one, not yet the interstate's.
1939: Bureau of Public Roads report *Toll Roads and Free Roads*, requested by Roosevelt, recommends a national network of free, limited-access highways.
1944: Federal-Aid Highway Act designates the National System of Interstate Highways on the map, with no money attached.
1945: Eisenhower, now in Germany, drives the Reichsautobahn. He files the impression for later use.
1956: Federal-Aid Highway Act signed by President Eisenhower, June 29. Twenty-five billion dollars, 41,000 miles, 90 percent federal funding. The Highway Trust Fund is created, paid for by the federal gas tax. The certified design standard is national; the procedure is uniform across the states.
1957: First contract awarded; first interstate section opened, in Missouri, on I-70.
1960: Joan Didion writes from Sacramento about the freeway as a state of mind; the freeway is already a metaphor.
1961: Eisenhower's farewell address. The interstate is in his short list of accomplishments, alongside the warning about the military-industrial complex.
1962: Federal Highway Beautification Act precursor debates; Lady Bird Johnson signs the final act in 1965, limiting billboards and junkyards along the right of way.
1973: Oil embargo. The federal speed limit is set at 55 miles per hour to save fuel. *Drive 55* on the speedometer; truckers ignore it on the CB.
1980s: The freeway revolt. San Francisco refuses to complete I-480 along the Embarcadero. New York never finishes the Lower Manhattan Expressway. Boston starts planning to bury I-93 underground.
1987: National Maximum Speed Limit raised to 65 on rural interstates; in 1995 the federal regulation on speed is repealed entirely, and states set their own.
1990: The system is officially renamed for Eisenhower.
1991: Intermodal Surface Transportation Efficiency Act (ISTEA). The first time federal highway money is allowed to be spent on transit, bike paths, and rail.
2005: Boston's Big Dig opens to traffic; the elevated Central Artery comes down, I-93 runs underground through downtown.
2021: Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act. About 110 billion dollars for roads and bridges, with a specific line for reconnecting neighborhoods that the original interstate program cut in half.
2024: The system carries about a quarter of all US vehicle miles traveled on about one percent of the road mileage. The original 41,000 miles is long since exceeded; maintenance, not construction, is now the main bill.

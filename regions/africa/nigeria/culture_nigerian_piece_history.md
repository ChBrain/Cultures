# History: Nigeria

## Owner
- Project: Cultures
- Culture: Nigeria

## Place
Nigeria.
West Africa, between the Sahel and the Atlantic, between the Niger and the Cross River.
A geography that held some of the most sophisticated civilisations in human history before it held a name given by a foreign journalist.
The land carries layers: Nok terracotta, Benin bronze, Yoruba iron, Igbo republican governance, Hausa-Fulani Islamic scholarship, British colonial administration, oil, military, democracy.
The borders drawn in 1914 contain over 500 languages and three civilisations that had been distinct for a thousand years.
The map is colonial. The people predate it by millennia.

## Load Bearing
Without this arc the [Nigerian](culture_nigerian_position.md) position loses its ground.
The circle is not a response to colonial failure. It is older than the colony by a thousand years.
The Benin Kingdom maintained a continuous court, a continuous art tradition, and a continuous system of governance from the ninth century. The bronzes were not primitive. They were the most technically sophisticated metal casting in the world at the time of their making.
The Igbo republican tradition -- stateless, merit-based, governed through the Oha na Eze -- is the oldest model of the circle as governance unit on the continent.
The Oyo Empire at its height administered a territory larger than Britain through a system of checks on royal power that had no European equivalent in the same century.
The Sokoto Caliphate built a federated Islamic state across the north that survived British conquest by transformation, not erasure.
The continuity of these systems still shapes the present map of authority.
Colonialism interrupted a civilisation. It did not begin one.
The oil years gave the state revenue without accountability and taught a generation that institutions serve those who control them, not those who built them.
The 1999 return to civilian rule is the current ground. Fragile, contested, still running.
The history carries because it has not been told in full. Telling it in full is itself a political act.

## Apparent
Nok terracotta figures in the National Museum Lagos and Jos: seated, standing, hollow-headed, 2500 years old.
Benin bronzes in the Benin Royal Palace -- and in the British Museum, the Ethnologisches Museum Berlin, the Pitt Rivers Museum Oxford. Repatriation negotiations ongoing.
The walls of Benin: the largest earthwork in the world before industrial growth, seen in aerial photos across Edo State.
Osun-Osogbo Sacred Grove, UNESCO: continuous Yoruba sacred practice, still active.
The National War Museum in Umuahia: Biafran memorabilia, photographs, the silence around them.
The National Assembly complex in Abuja: built on the plateau, visible from the highway, glass and concrete.
Nnamdi Azikiwe International Airport, Murtala Muhammed International Airport: names of founding fathers on infrastructure.
Afrobeats on every phone, in every market, in clubs from Lagos to London to São Paulo.
The naira note: the portraits change with governments. The geometry stays.

## Yearbook
500 BCE: Nok culture flourishes on the Jos Plateau. Terracotta sculpture, iron smelting. The oldest known figurative art tradition in sub-Saharan Africa.
900 CE: Benin Kingdom established in the forest zone. Continuous court culture, palace system, bronze casting tradition that will run for a thousand years.
1000: Kanem-Bornu Empire consolidates around Lake Chad. Trans-Saharan trade routes. Islamic scholarship enters the north through commerce before conquest.
1100: Yoruba city-states emerge. Ile-Ife as spiritual centre. The Ooni as sacred authority. Urban civilisation in the forest.
1400: Oyo Empire rises. Cavalry. Trade. A constitutional check on royal power through the Oyo Mesi council. The Alafin can be asked to die by his own chiefs.
1440: Oba Ewuare the Great expands Benin Kingdom. The palace bronzes reach their classical period. Diplomatic contact with Portugal begins within fifty years.
1485: Portuguese arrive at Benin. They find a court that receives them as equals, conducts diplomacy, controls trade on its own terms. They want pepper and ivory. Benin wants guns and copper.
1500s: Atlantic slave trade begins its expansion. The Niger Delta kingdoms -- Calabar, Bonny, Brass -- become intermediaries. The trade reshapes politics, economics, and kinship structures across the region for three centuries.
1804: Usman dan Fodio launches the Fulani Jihad. The Sokoto Caliphate is established across the north. Federated Islamic governance, scholarship, and law. At its height, the largest state in Africa.
1807: Britain abolishes the slave trade. The Royal Navy begins patrols. The economy of the Delta kingdoms transforms toward palm oil. British commercial interest intensifies.
1851: British bombardment of Lagos. The Oba deposed. A treaty state installed. The beginning of formal colonial encroachment.
1861: Lagos annexed as a British colony. The first formal territorial claim.
1884: Berlin Conference. European powers divide Africa. Nigeria's boundaries are drawn by people who have not seen the land.
1900: British Protectorates of Northern and Southern Nigeria formally declared. The Sokoto Caliphate falls to Frederick Lugard's forces. Indirect rule begins: colonial administration through existing traditional authority.
1914: Amalgamation. Northern and Southern Nigeria joined into one administrative unit by Lugard. The name Nigeria, coined by journalist Flora Shaw, becomes official. Three civilisations under one colonial border.
1929: Women's War (Igbo: Ogu Umunwanyi). Igbo women rise against British taxation and colonial interference in markets. Tens of thousands march. The colonial administration shoots. Dozens die. The circle defends itself.
1944: National Council of Nigeria and the Cameroons founded by Nnamdi Azikiwe. The first mass nationalist political organisation. Independence becomes a question of when, not if.
1960: Independence. 1 October. Tafawa Balewa becomes Prime Minister. Nigeria enters the world as the most populous country in Africa, with the largest educated class on the continent.
1966: First military coup. January. The democratic experiment is six years old. Counter-coup in July. The ethnicity of the coup-makers becomes the story. The federal structure fractures.
1967: Biafra declares independence. The southeast, predominantly Igbo, secedes. Federal blockade. Starvation as weapon. One million to three million dead in thirty months.
1970: Biafra falls. "No victor, no vanquished." The reconciliation is declared. It is not complete. It is not complete now.
1973: Oil boom. OPEC price rises. Nigeria's oil revenue multiplies. The state becomes an oil state: revenue without accountability, patronage without development. The oga network scales to the national level.
1979: Return to civilian rule. Second Republic. Shehu Shagari elected. The experiment lasts four years.
1983: Military coup. Muhammadu Buhari seizes power. Austerity. Decree No. 4: imprisonment for publishing information embarrassing to the government. The press learns the cost of the formal register.
1985: Ibrahim Babangida displaces Buhari. Structural Adjustment Programme imposed under IMF pressure. Subsidies removed. Naira devalued. A generation learns that institutions will not hold them. The circle tightens.
1993: June 12. Moshood Abiola wins the presidential election. The most credible result in Nigerian history. Babangida annuls it. Mass protests. Abiola imprisoned. He dies in detention in 1998. June 12 becomes a wound and a symbol simultaneously.
1995: Ken Saro-Wiwa executed by the Abacha regime. Writer, activist, Ogoni rights defender. The Niger Delta has been producing the oil. The Niger Delta has received the pollution and none of the revenue. The execution shocks the world. Nigeria is suspended from the Commonwealth.
1999: Return to civilian rule. Olusegun Obasanjo elected. The Fourth Republic begins. It is still running. Fragile, contested, still running.
2000s: Nollywood becomes the second largest film industry in the world by volume. Nigeria tells its own stories, in its own languages, for its own audience, and the audience is continental and global. The screen is the new circle.
2012: #OccupyNigeria. Fuel subsidy removal. Millions on the streets. The protest is real. The subsidy is partially restored. The lesson: the circle can become a crowd.
2015: Muhammadu Buhari wins the presidential election. The first time in Nigerian history that an incumbent peacefully transfers power to an opposition candidate. The institution holds, once.
2020: #EndSARS. Youth-led protests against police brutality. The Special Anti-Robbery Squad dissolved after years of extrajudicial killings. The Lekki toll gate. Soldiers fire on protesters. The state and its people in the same posture as 1929. The circle remembers.
2023: Bola Tinubu elected president. Petrol subsidy removed on inauguration day. The naira floats. The cost of daily life doubles. The Japa wave accelerates. A generation calculates whether to stay or go.
